Underwriting Optimization & Risk Strategy

Redesigned top-of-funnel targeting and risk policies to reduce both acquisition cost and lifetime losses—without sacrificing growth.

Situation

The problem

During the 1→10 growth phase, we ran multiple experiments across acquisition, underwriting, and portfolio management to understand customer behavior and key risk drivers. As the business scaled beyond 10, the focus shifted to profitable, sustainable growth. The challenge was that CAC and portfolio losses were rising together. Tightening credit policy reduced losses but slowed growth, while pursuing aggressive growth increased risk. The objective was to identify customer segments and strategy changes that could reduce both acquisition costs and credit losses simultaneously, improving unit economics without compromising growth.

Approach

What I did

  • Ran univariate and multivariate analysis to isolate high-risk, unprofitable microsegments; excluding the worst ~10% of these microsegments cut portfolio losses by 15%.
  • Profiled CAC by acquisition channel and policy segment (low to high) and found the highest-CAC segments also had the lowest conversion and negligible portfolio contribution. Redirected the 25% of CAC spend tied up in these segments toward higher-converting channels, with no loss in bottom-line volume.
  • Designed a risk-based gap funding strategy — approving marginal-funding-gap applicants backed by strong probability-of-default confidence via partial-funding structures and pre-payment rates — lifting approval rates 10% without degrading expected loss.
  • Reinvested the freed-up spend and underwriting capacity into a refinance product pilot, opening a new low-risk growth channel that fully offset the volume lost from segment pruning.

EXHIBIT 3 · Segmentation methodology

Univariate screening flagged single-variable drivers of loss (geography, channel, risk grade). Multivariate screening then tested interactions between those variables to confirm compounding risk, narrowing the candidate list down to the ~10% of the tape ultimately excluded — the cohort responsible for the 15% loss reduction.

Outcome

Result

The three levers reinforced each other: pruning unprofitable microsegments freed capital, the freed CAC funded the refinance pilot instead of disappearing from the budget, and the pilot's growth offset what pruning cost the top of funnel. The net effect wasn't a smaller, more conservative book — it was the same growth trajectory on a cheaper, better-performing base of loans.

Takeaways

What I'd flag for next time

Getting the analysis right was half the work — the other half was translating "we're excluding these segments" into something growth and product could get behind. That meant multiple rounds of stakeholder discussion across growth, product, and business leadership, where the real question was never "is this segment risky" but "how much risk reduction justifies how much growth given up." Sitting in those conversations shifted how I think about tradeoffs generally — every function in a business is optimizing a different variable, and the tradeoff economics between risk and growth aren't unique to lending; they show up in some form everywhere. That's the piece that felt like it moved me from doing the analysis to owning the decision.
