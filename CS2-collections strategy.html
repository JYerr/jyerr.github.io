Collections Strategy — Personal Loans & BNPL

Segmenting a fast-growing loan book by early-warning risk, so collections effort lands on the right accounts before they're even past due — not after.

Situation

The problem

A fast-growing personal loan and BNPL book meant the collections team couldn't treat every past-due account the same way — there wasn't enough capacity to chase everyone equally, and chasing everyone equally wastes effort on accounts that would have cured on their own. The brief was to get ahead of risk before the due date, not just react after an account bounced.

Approach

What I did

  • Built a decision-tree based segmentation model using repayment history, cross-sell exposure, and acquisition-risk signals to classify accounts into priority tiers ahead of the due date.
  • Split the strategy into a pre-due track (proactive outreach on the highest-risk segment before an account was ever late) and a post-due track (reactive handling once an account had bounced).
  • Iterated the model from an account-level view to a relationship-level view — collections works at the level of the whole customer relationship, so an account that looked healthy on its own could still sit inside a risky relationship.
  • Separately built a hybrid model for another portfolio — rule-based tree logic where bucket volume was large enough to support stable splits, and a scorecard built from a group of micro bad-signals for the late-stage buckets where volume thinned out too much for a rule-based split to hold up.

EXHIBIT 2 · Two methodologies, matched to the portfolio
MethodBest fit forHow it prioritizes
Decision tree Fast-growing book with rich behavioral history Splits accounts into discrete risk tiers via rule-based branching — easy to explain and act on operationally.
Scorecard Tail segments where tree buckets get too thin to stay statistically stable Blends many weighted micro-signals into one continuous score, so no single split needs a large sample to be reliable.

Model choice came down to sample stability, not a preference for simplicity — the scorecard often draws on more signals than the tree, it just doesn't need enough volume within any one combination of them.

Anticipated question · "How do we know the thin-bucket scorecard actually works?"

Validated the late-stage scorecard on its own, not blended into portfolio-wide numbers — a strong tree segment can otherwise mask a weak scorecard segment. Used resampled confidence intervals given how small that population is, checked performance held on a later time window (not just a random held-out split), and benchmarked against what recovery looked like on that tail before the scorecard existed, since "better than nothing" was the more honest bar than "better than the tree" for a segment the tree couldn't reliably score in the first place.

Validation

How the models were checked

A tiering strategy is only as good as its ability to hold up on accounts the model hasn't seen yet — both models were validated against held-out data, not just the population they were built on. The metrics below split into two families that answer different questions. KS and AUC are built by sweeping every possible score cutoff and checking how well the model ranks bad accounts above good ones at each one — they say whether the model can separate risk at all, before any operating decision gets made. Accuracy, Precision, and Sensitivity are read at one chosen cutoff instead, and are sensitive to how rare bad accounts are in the book — they say what actually happens once that cutoff gets picked. A model needs to look good on both: strong ranking power that falls apart at the cutoff collections can staff to isn't useful, and neither is a cutoff-specific number with no ranking power behind it.

Outcome

Result

Focusing pre-due outreach on the highest-risk tier cut bounce rates by roughly 20% and lifted recovery by around 15%, without a proportionate increase in collections headcount. The complementary scorecard model brought a similar lift to a second portfolio using a lighter-weight approach better suited to its data.

Takeaways

What I'd flag for next time

The model type mattered as much as the variables inside it — a decision tree earns its keep when there's enough population at every leaf to make each split stable; a scorecard earns its keep once that population thins out, since it can lean on many weighted micro-signals without needing volume behind any single combination of them. Matching the method to where the data actually held up was as important as any individual feature.

The part that's easy to underweight: none of this mattered until collections actually adopted it. Getting there took a simple one-page download of what was built, why, and what impact to expect — not the modeling detail, just enough for the team to trust and act on it. The decision tree carried real weight here beyond its accuracy: because its splits map onto plain rules a non-technical stakeholder can trace by eye, it was far easier to walk the collections team through, get their buy-in, and iterate on together than the scorecard was — which mattered more for adoption than any marginal gain in model performance.
