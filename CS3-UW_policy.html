Case File 2026-CF03

Underwriting Policy Innovation — Top-Up & Subprime Lending

Extending credit in two different directions at once — deeper into existing good customers through a top-up product, and cautiously into higher-risk applicants through a subprime product — without loosening the underlying risk discipline either way.

Situation

Two different gaps in the funnel

Two separate problems, both underwriting gaps rather than acquisition ones. First: existing customers who'd proven themselves creditworthy had no path to more credit without reapplying from scratch, even when their risk profile had visibly improved. Second: applicants who fell just under standard bureau cutoffs got a blanket decline, even when many of them had strong repayment history on an existing on-book relationship that the bureau score alone didn't capture.

Approach

What I did

  • Built a top-up policy for existing personal loan customers, anchored on how much a customer's credit score had moved since their first loan (more on this below), a clean recent delinquency record, and a minimum loan vintage — so top-ups went to borrowers who'd demonstrably gotten safer, not just older.
  • Derived new top-up loan amount and tenure as a multiplier off each customer's original loan terms and current risk tier, rather than re-underwriting from zero — kept pricing and limits tied to the live acquisition-policy grid instead of standing up a separate parallel one.
  • Built a subprime (high-yield) policy for applicants who'd normally be declined on bureau score alone, gated on strong repayment behavior from an existing on-book relationship — approved with tighter limits, shorter tenures, and risk-adjusted pricing instead of a flat rejection.
  • Layered secured-asset context — home loan, auto loan, and credit card limits — into a separate top-tier segmentation on the acquisition side, since bureau score alone was under-pricing genuinely low-risk, asset-rich borrowers.
Analysis

How eligibility actually gets decided

EXHIBIT 1 · Bureau Drift — the core top-up signal
Bureau Drift = Latest Bureau Score − Acquisition Bureau Score
+100 — score moved 700 → 800 since the first loan. Reads as: this customer has gotten meaningfully safer.
−100 — score moved 800 → 700 since the first loan. Reads as: this customer has gotten meaningfully riskier, top-up excluded regardless of other criteria.

Positive drift plus a clean recent delinquency record is the anchor of top-up eligibility — it's a direct read on "has this specific customer's risk changed," rather than relying on a static score threshold that treats everyone the same regardless of trajectory.

EXHIBIT 2 · Two policies, two different risk logics
PolicyWho it's forWhat gates eligibility
Top-Up Existing customers in good standing Positive bureau drift, clean recent delinquency, minimum loan vintage — proving the customer got safer, not just older.
Subprime (High-Yield) New applicants below standard bureau cutoffs Strong on-book repayment behavior on an existing relationship, overriding a bureau score that alone would decline them — priced tighter to offset the added risk.

Same underlying goal — price risk accurately instead of using a single blunt cutoff — applied in opposite directions of the funnel.

Reference

Bureau & banking signals this kind of policy leans on

These are the variable families that actually drive decisions like the ones above — grouped by what each one is really asking, not just what it's called.

EXHIBIT 3 · Bureau signals
SignalWhy it matters
Recent large credit openings New sizeable lines opened elsewhere in a short recent window — a customer taking on debt fast right before your decision is a red flag your own bureau pull alone won't explain.
Unsecured enquiry volume Shopping around specifically for unsecured credit correlates with near-term cash need more than secured-loan shopping does — it's often a leading indicator ahead of stress showing up anywhere else.
Secured vs. unsecured enquiry mix Enquiries skewed toward unsecured products read as a stronger risk signal than the same count skewed toward secured ones — unsecured debt is what people reach for first under pressure.
Enquiry-to-approval ratio Many enquiries but few resulting loans means other lenders are declining this customer — a signal your own model might not otherwise see, since it only sees your own outcome.
Recent non-card delinquency (DPD) Missed payments on installment loans specifically, excluding cards — card delinquency behaves differently and more commonly, so blending it in dilutes the signal.
High-credit sanction amount, recent & cumulative How much credit this customer has already been trusted with, recently and over their lifetime — a proxy for both their profile's scale and how much headroom is left before overexposure.
Total & active account count Breadth of credit history (experience) versus currently live obligations (how stretched they already are) — two different questions from what looks like one number.
EXHIBIT 4 · Banking signals
SignalWhy it matters
Bounce & return entries Cheque/ACH bounces, their charges, minimum-balance charges, and internal transfers get stripped out before computing income — left in, they distort the genuine cash-flow read a decision actually needs.
Monthly transaction activity Debit/credit counts per month over a recent window — a very low count suggests a dormant or pass-through account rather than someone's real primary bank account, which weakens any income estimate pulled from it.
Inward cheque / EMI return count The banking-side equivalent of bureau delinquency — it can catch repayment stress that hasn't reached the bureau yet, since bureau reporting always lags real account activity.
Income-to-EMI affordability correlation A sanity check between declared income and what the bureau shows the customer actually paying out each month — catches cases where the two stories don't agree.

Skip-banking logic: banking data is the more reliable affordability signal but the slowest, highest-friction one to collect — so it only gets skipped when the bureau profile alone is "thick" enough (sufficient account history, positive net disposable income, low-enough risk tier) to trust on its own.

Outcome

Result

The subprime cohort held a 90% on-time repayment rate — evidence that behavioral, on-book signals were a legitimate override for applicants a bureau-only cutoff would have declined outright. The top-up policy gave existing good customers a path to more credit without a full reunderwrite, while the bureau-drift gate kept that expansion tied to each customer's actual trajectory rather than a static score.

Takeaways

What I'd flag for next time

The recurring theme across both policies: a single bureau score at a single point in time is a blunt instrument in either direction — too generous to customers who've quietly gotten riskier, too harsh to applicants a static cutoff misjudges. Trajectory (bureau drift) and behavior (on-book repayment history) were both more useful than the score alone, just applied on opposite ends of the funnel.
